    Chapter 3 MA123 Quantitative Literacy

    In Quantitative Literacy, you will learn how to read, understand, and interpret the numerical data that permeates our lives. You will apply problem-solving skills to make financial decisions, use proportional reasoning to convert units, apply your knowledge of percentages to multistage processes, apply the appropriate amount of precision in making claims, recognize the difference between linear and exponential growth, and use two-way tables to analyze false negatives and false positives. You will become aware of how mathematics may be used by others to mislead you and how to ensure that you are interpreting and presenting your results honestly and accurately.
    f you are not majoring in Nursing, Education, or a quantitative field, you have freedom in selecting the mathematics course to satisfy your general education requirement. The course described here focuses on applying your algebra skills to every day life decisions. For some students, this is an ideal course as they gain useful tools and appreciate math through its application. Other students may already be familiar with these applications and find this course to be less useful. Read on to see and try sample problems.
    Other courses that may be used to fulfill the general education mathematics requirement are MA126 Puzzles, Patterns, and Probability, MA127 Geometrical Explorations, and MA130 Functions. Click on the links to learn more about these courses.

    Section 3.1 Am I ready for MA123 Quantitative Literacy?

    Before taking MA123, students will be expected to have prior proficiency in performing operations on fractions, simplifying algebraic expressions, solving linear equations, and working with percents and decimals. Normally, the use of a calculator is allowed in this course, but students need to be able to convert word problems to algebraic equations, recognizing how to input values and solve for the appropriate variable. Students needing to strengthen these skills are encouraged to consider starting in MA100 Math Refresher. To test your readiness for MA123, you are encouraged to attempt the problems in Exercises¬†2.1 and then check your work. You do not need to be able to do the problems below in Section¬†3.2; you will learn these skills and concepts during the course.

    Section 3.2 What will I learn in MA123 Quantitative Literacy?

    At the end of MA123 (not now!), you should be able to do the following types of exercises. Calculators are allowed unless specified. These problems are provided to give you a sense of what you will learn in this course. You need only try enough of these problems to check that this course will not be too easy and repetitive for you. If you do complete problems, you are encouraged to view the provided answers and solutions after (not before) you finish each group of problems.

    Exercises Exercises

    1. Reasoning proportionally and convert between units.
    The cost of a liter of gasoline in Brasilia in June 2023 was 5.23 Brazilian Real. Determine the equivalent cost in US dollars per gallon. Use the exchange rate 1 B Real = 0.21 US Dollar and the conversion factor 1 liter = 0.264 gallon in your work. Round to the nearest penny per gallon.
    Hint.
    How do we define being able to do this problem? Speed is less important that reasoning it out. Can you set it up and use your knowledge of multiplication and division of fractions? Searching for similar problems to mimic, looking at the solution, or randomly trying operations does not count as knowledge of the topic.
    Answer.
    4.16 US dollars per gallon
    Solution.
    \(\frac{5.23 \text{BRL}}{1 \text{liter}}\cdot\frac{0.21 \text{USD}}{1\text{BRL}} \cdot\frac{1 \text{liter}}{0.264\text{gal}}\)
    2. Understanding and using percentages.
    (a)
    Write \(64\%\) as a simplified fraction.
    Answer.
    \(\frac{16}{25}\)
    (b)
    If you take a 5 percent cut in pay this year and then get a 10 percent raise the following year, by what percent will your salary grow over the two-year period?
    Select from the following:
    1. 2.5 %
    2. 4.5 %
    3. 5.0 %
    4. 7.5 %
    Hint.
    What if you are making $100,000?
    Answer.
    b. Your salary will grow by 4.5 percent. Note that 5.0 percent is incorrect since percent growth is not additive.
    Solution.
    If your salary at the beginning of the problem is \(S\text{,}\) then your salary will be \(0.95S\) after the first year and \(1.10(0.95S)=(1.1\cdot 0.95)S=1.045S\) after the second year.
    (c)
    Your restaurant bill of $55.59 includes a 9% meals tax. What was the charge for the meal before the tax was added? Your answer should be correct to the nearest cent.
    Answer.
    $51.00. If you got $50.59, you made the common mistake of subtracting \(0.9\cdot 55.59\) from $55.59. Check your work by increasing your answer by 9 percent. Do you get $55.59?
    Solution.
    The unknown quantity in this problem is the amount \(A\) before the 9% tax is added. We solve \(A+0.9A=50.59\) which can be rewritten as \(1.09A=50.59\text{.}\)
    3. Use scientific notation effectively.
    (a)
    Your calculator displays the number \(5.147284\text{E}5\) which is equivalent to \(5.147284\times 10^5\text{.}\) Which of the following best describes the magnitude of this number?
    1. The number is between 5 and 6.
    2. The number is between 10 and 1000.
    3. The number is between 50000 and 60000.
    4. The number is between 500000 and 600000.
    Answer.
    d. The number is 514,728.4. You should be able to do this type of exercise without a calculator.
    (b)
    Perform the following operations without a calculator. Express your answers in scientific notation.
    1. \(\displaystyle (4\times 10^6)\times (2\times 10^2)\)
    2. \(\displaystyle (4\times 10^6)+(2\times 10^2)\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \frac{4\times 10^6}{2\times 10^2}\)
    Answer.
    (a) \(8\times 10^8\text{;}\) (b) \(4.0002\times 10^6\text{;}\) (c) \(2\times 10^4\)
    Solution.
    (a) \((4\times 10^6)\times (2\times 10^2)=(4\cdot 2)\times (10^{6+2})=8\times10^8\text{;}\) (b) \((4\times 10^6)+(2\times 10^2)=4000000+200=4000200=4.0002\times 10^6\text{;}\) \(\frac{4\times 10^6}{2\times 10^2}=\frac{4}{2}\times 10^{6-2}=2\times 10^4\)
    4. Use mathematics to make wise financial decisions.
    1. Prorate income sources and expenses to monthly and determine the net monthly income or loss for an individual.
    2. Determine whether an insurance policy would be a good deal by weighing the overall cost of the policy against estimated out-of-pocket expenses without a policy.
    3. Argue whether it is better, both financially and economically, to trade your old car for a new one.
    Hint.
    Answering these questions would require more information that is given here. These examples are included to illustrate how many of the problems in this course involve more research and reasoning and connections to our daily life.
    5. Use two-way tables to analyze probabilities.
    A positive test should indicate that a person has the disease and a negative test should indicate that a person does not have the disease. These tests unfortunately are not 100% accurate.
    The following table breaks down a sample of 100 people who were tested for Disease L according to whether they had Disease L and whether the test indicated that they had the disease. Use the table to answer the questions that follow:
    1. If a patient's test indicates that they have the disease, what is the probability that they actually do not have Disease L?
    2. If a patient does not have Disease L, what is the probability that the test will confirm that they do not have the disease?
    Answer.
    (a) 20 percent; (b) 97.73 percent
    Solution.
    For b, we divide 86 by 88. Knowing how to do this type of problem means that you can read the two-way table and determine which values you need to divide to determine the probability. In addition, you may be asked to complete a partially-filled table.
    6. Recognize whether growth is linear or exponential and solve problems involving each type.
    A city in Texas had a population of 100000 in January 2020 and was growing at a rate of 6% per year.
    1. What is the approximate doubling time at this growth rate?
    2. If the growth rate were to continue, what would the city's population be in 2050?
    Answer.
    (a) approximately 11.9 years; (b) nearly 574,350
    Solution.
    1. We solve the equation \(2P=P1.06^{t}\text{.}\) We can divide both sides by \(P\text{,}\) leaving \(2=1.06^{t}\text{.}\) One way to complete this problem is to graph \(y=1.06^x\) and \(y=2\) on the same coordinate graph (using technology) to see where the curves intersect. To solve this by computations, we would take a logarithm (log or ln) of both sides to get \(t=\frac{log(2)}{log(1.06)}\approx11.9\text{.}\)
    2. \(1000000\cdot1.06^{50-20}\approx574,349\text{.}\)
